"Who," asked the brilliant teacher, "is the Christian hero?" Is there still a need and a call for Christian heroism? No one will ask the question whether or not Christian heroism was called for during the time of Nero, the bloodiest and most cruel of Roman emperors.

"One of the forms of torture resorted to by Nero was to gather a number of the Christians in his garden, have them dressed for the special purpose, oiled and set on fire, while he and his friends looked on from the tower of his palace. These cruel practices furnished much the same excitement, sport and pleasure for the emperor and his chieftains, as you and I experience when we go to a ball game and watch with interest the star players, the priests and the laughter or tears, when we go to a theater and follow the portrayal of a story of romance, of heroism, or of tragedy.

"There was, indeed, in the infancy of the Christian church, a call for sublime heroism; not such as sends the soldier to battle, the explorer to the perils of an unknown sea, or the hazard of the warrior in the attempt to conquer a hostile country; not such as men endure for mere honor and fame, that rare, superlative heroism, like a rock and, if need be, to stand like a rock and, if need be, to die for his convictions. Such was the heroism of the early Christians.

"If now, as then, to be a Christian meant imprisonment, execution, the rack or the stake, how many of us, of us, are bound by the courage to say, I am a Christian?"

"What happened when Luther and his contemporaries took the stand for a purer church and state? Like an irresistible glacier they swept everything that crossed their path. By their teachings and their heroism, Europe as by a succession of volcanic eruptions was stirred from center to circumference and became the seething life. Into that not were thrown the surviving elements of ancient civilization, a modification of all the religions and cults of previous ages. The religious and civil ideas and ideals of ancient Egypt as found in the hieroglyphics upon her pyramids, were collected there; the spirit of conquest and greed which characterized Assyria was there; the prophetic and ethical note which was struck by the israelitish prophets aided that process fusion; the imaginative, romantic, and political thinking of the Greek, reinforced by the discipline of the laws and armies of Rome, helped to make the Reformation necessary and inevitable; shot through and through by the spirit of Christ as the spirit of the age was, nothing less that revolution and reformation could follow. And do you ask what happened? Out of that seething pot was molded a new type of thought; out of the jumbrelles of restlessness, confusion and war, sprang a new day, and were carved the present Christian nations of Europe and the great American Republic; it was the dawning of a new era. As a result of and along with the Reformation the revival of learning broke forth; the spirit of invention and discovery had its birth; by this mighty movement the missionary zeal and enthusiasm which had been put to sleep by the vanity, pride and ambition of popes and prelates, was again kindled in the hearts and minds of the people, and, as during the Reformation, many were gathered into the Christian church by the hundreds and thousands and the Protestant churches began their great mission of Christianizing the nations of the earth.

Beginning with this issue the Defender will publish, exclusively, a series of sermons delivered by Dr. C. Lee Jefferson, of Hope Presbyterian church.

The topics which he has selected are grand, noble and inspiring. They have upon the home and all phases of home life; and coming from the eloquent pen of Dr. Jefferson, they will be read with much interest.

The Ideal Home.

"Home is the grandest of all institutions."—S spurgeon.

This is a subject of vital importance to every true man and woman; a subject which lies close to the heart of every patriotic American. There are about 20,000,000 homes in the United States and about 6,000,000 are

This being a democratic government instead of a monarchy, the family, or home, is exalted, and therefore the court of highest appeal in all that pertains to our national welfare.

And especially does this subject greatly concern every Afro-American, because we were launched upon the old ship of state practically without a ship of state. This soliton fact has been too often of consideration both by our friends and by ouroes. Hence we have been left largely to the caprice of unregulated pride, lust and appetite in the building of our homes.

The essential: being of a Christian family is the legitimate union of one man and one woman in holy wedlock. The true family is the oldest human institution in the world—had its origin in the Garden of Eden and in the temple. It has been the forerunner of all succeeding institutions, organizations and civilizations throughout the ages, whenever and wherever it has been allowed to develop along natural lines. Man is born in society; he is an offspring of the community; he begins his career in the community and he can only develop his highest powers for good and efficiency in the community. Man lives to himself alone, hence any scheme that tends towards the development and perfection of his moral, intellectual and spiritual powers must include a recognition of this society unit—the family. This is the indestructible unit. Destroy the nation, state and the tribe; if you spare the family all will be replaced. Desolation may sweep from the earth all systems of government, nations and tribes, but so long as the family remains their reconstruction is successful, growing spring from whence flows all the sparkling waters of national life.

"Language was given for the concealment of thought," said the readymade philosopher. "Yes," replied Miss Cayenne. "Many an impropriety is hidden by a scientific word of four or five syllables."
