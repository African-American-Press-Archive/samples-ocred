Fillmore's Resignation

Whether the resignation of Lieut. Col. Charles W. Fillmore will act to better the regiment is not known just now. Fillmore has always been identified with the 15th. Since his enlistment in the national guard in the mid-1870s he has always been connected with the military in some way. His first commission was with the Ninth battalion of Ohio and his last with the new 15th. His resignation as second lieutenant and pieces he placed in his service officer still lie in the of the guard. He won citations for service during the world war in France and won the croix de guerre on Dec. 6, 1918. Muj. Chislom, who is now acting as executive officer of the regiment, has a fine personality may do something to bring the regiment up from the present depths to which it has gone down.

Success Irritates Some People

For some time New York has been flooded with stock-selling schemes of all nature. Most of the promotors of these schemes try to sell their stock by appealing to Race loyalty: pride, joy. Few, however, ever pay any dividends, and the real reason for investing money. The record of money lost in stock buying is appalling. One concern in New York, however, is actually paying dividends—the Berry & Ross Company. The company pays regular three months' dividends, but an annual has been declared and will be paid the latter part of May. The success of the Berry & Ross people, who first entered into business with the famous Colored dolls, seems to have irritated New York to the end that recently one of them, who, by the way, has floated one of the most gigantic stock selling schemes over put out among our people, referred to the dolls of Berry & Ross as bigger dolls. The attitude of the company never declared any dividends on its stock—never talks dividend, in fact, seems to be one of sour graps.

THE CHICAGO DEFENDER

Many Attend Monster Meeting

Seven hundred people attended the concert of the joint organization movement for the enforcement of the 14th and 15th amendments held at Carnegie Hall last Thursday night, under the auspices of a combination of literary, war, and elite societies. The meeting was held civic society. The event was for the enforcement of the two amendments. Among the speakers who told of the accomplishments of the Race, especially after the abolition of slavery, and of many valorous words in the more recent world war were Rev. R. A. Porter, Mrs. Lawton, Rev. W. H. Hurte, Prof. H. T. Harvey, Hon. Andrew B. Humphrey (white), and Hon. George B. Sims. It was announced from the platform that T. Walker, known as the "Black Spurgeon," of Augusta, Ga., who was born in Boston, spoke, would not be present on account of pressing business in his home. A committee of three, consisting of Counselor A. B. Cozey, Messrs. Handolph and Hinton, were appointed after a motion had been passed to hear a resolution to be sent to every congressional member of the United States and to both the Republican and Democrat national conventions at Chicago and Sun Francisco. Letters were read by Mme. M. Waller French from Mayor E. P. Smith of Chicago, Neb.: Senator Harding of Chicago, Neb.: Senator Harding of Chicago, Neb.: J. Alen of Kansas, all expressing therein the views of the enforcement of the amendments. Among those who took part in the concert were Mme. Elizabeth W. Mayfield, who recited the Battle Hymn of the Republic: little Miss Gr鲁德儿 R. violin solo, accompanied by Miss A. R. piano. The 14th regiment band furnished music for the evening. The audience contributed $302.50 to carry on the good work.
