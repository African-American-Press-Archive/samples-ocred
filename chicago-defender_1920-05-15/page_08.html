ALL OF TEXAS HEARS SIMMONS

Houston, Tex., May 14.—Closing a tour of three weeks in Texas, Regison Simmons left the state for North. He spoke to students at 10,000 people, in many places to audiences equally divided as to race; never to any except a mixed audience.

Flaya Jim Crow Car

On this trip the spokesman for the common people jumped on the Jim Crow car, saying to the white people that the Jim Crow car had locked behind its doors the honor of a country. "Let me out," he said, "and honor will walk out with me." Col. Simmons spoke under arrangements made by William McDonald and James B. Grissby, big men among the real big men of Texas. No place on his tour, except the Houston Auditorium, was big enough to accommodate the crowds that wanted to listen to Col. Simmons "lay out" the illly whites. "To say that he advanced the cause is hardly saying anything, for Col. Simmons is alone in his work, both in the extent and character of it.

Simmons Praised

No such compliment was ever paid a member of the Race as that paid Col. Simmons by the pen of the brilliant Henry Ellis, editor of the Dentson, Tex., Morning Herald, who, after hearing Col. Simmons, wrote in his paper these words:

"In word-pictures rarely heard, with pathos just enough, sparkling humor and native wit, he kept his audience as long as he wished. So well was his appeal to the Negro put that they remain true to the flag, and to America; so touching was he in his description of the ideals to which he would have lived. He kept himself somewhat modifying the ancient words of King Agrippa, 'Almost thou persuadist me to be a Nogro.'"

In almost every city visited, Col. Simmons was welcomed by the mayor, and in several towns he was asked to include white high schools in his rounds.

Heaviest Woman Dies

Lancaster, Pa., May 14--Mrs. Lizzie Clark, aged 54, of 419 North street, who died here last week from dropsy, was privately buried from her home. She weighed 500 pounds at the time of her death. Despite her reputation of being the heaviest woman in the state, and the difficulty she had in getting from place to place as a result of her ailment, which caused her abnormal weight, she continued her hercolent work among her people.

The body was buried in a specially built casket twice the size of the average coffin.
