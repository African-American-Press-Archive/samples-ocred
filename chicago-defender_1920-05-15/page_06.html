This whirlwind honeymoon farce which is funnier than "Baby Mine," more miffful than "Twin Reds" and more laughable than "Fair and Warmer," ran for 30 consecutive weeks last season at the Fulton theater, New York, where it was procured at no small expense by the Quality Amusement Corporation production in the various theaters around its circuit.

Follow the side splitting experiences of an cloaking couple, an frate parent of the male sex, a camouflaged minister about to retire for the night, they make up a burglar, on the honeymoon resort where the bride and groom have planned to spend the first twenty-four hours of their honeymoon. But in the bridal suite of the mountain hotel, just as the honeymooners about to retire for the night, they have had a prohibition wedding, what they have had had has no kick to it; in other words, that their wedding was a filly. For they learn that the minister who officiated in the tying knot was bogus—a more burglar in disguise.

It is bad enough to be alone with a strange young woman on the first night of your honeymoon when you are really and truly married to her, but when you think you are and find you aren't that life's most embarrassing to you, you really want to be entertained, to you really want to real norriment, follow the honeymoon trip with the clooping couple in "Please Get Married."

"LOCKED LIPS"

At the States Theater next Sunday theater-goers of this city will be given an opportunity to see the Japanese Bernhardt, Tauri Aold, in her latest photodrama, "Locked Lips."

The success scored by Miss Aold in her first Universal production, "The Breath of the Gods," has established this Japanese actress as one of the foremost figures on the screen.

"Locked Lips" differs from any other photodrama not produced in which a Japanese character holds the center of the screen. Its scenes are not laid out plainly. There is no theme nor situation that is foreign to an American audience.

The story opens in a clubroom in an American city where Henry Stanwood, in a brawl over a game of cards, shoots a man and makes his escape. Fleeing from justice, his wanderers bring him to the island of Hilo. There he breaks into a cottage in search of food. It is home of Lotus Blossom, a Japanese girl who touched a native school. She nurses him back to health, strength and they are eventually married. The man's real nature soon asserts itself and he deserts Lotus Blossom.

His escape from the island, the changing of clothes with a body which had been washed ashore, his marriage to a girl in San Francisco and Blossom's real identity interrupted when she finds that her husband is married to the woman who had befriended her, are told in a series of gripping, tense situations that end in a breathless olimax.

The production shows the directorial of William C. Dowlan, while the star is requested by such players as Stanhope Wheateroff, Maigda Lane, Jack Abbey and others.

"DR. JEKYLL AND MR. HYDE"

Robert Louis Stevenson's powerful story, "Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde," has been translated to the screen with John Barrymore in the leading role and will be shown at the Vendome theater for three days, commencing on Thursday of the present week.

This is declared the most remarkable pictures over filmed, and the performance of Mr. Barrymore, it is claimed, is a worthy companion to that of Richard Mansfield, appeared in the same part in the stage play of Lord Macaulay.

The plot, as is well known, has its central figure a young London physician, Dr. Jekyll, whose tender-heartedness is exceeded only by his all-absorbing interest in his chosen profession. He is an esteemed inocent beautiful girl, Millicent Curew. Her father, Sir George Curew, is a famous man about town. Dr. Jekyll is persuaded by Sir George to accompany him to a low musical hall. From his study of his scientific investigations, he is appointed with a liquid that will separate the good and evil in a man into two distinct bodies. He evolves such a drug, administers it to himself and becomes not only Jekyll, the doctor-shaped, but also Hyde, a misshapen, villian god. Gradually, side of his character overmasters Jekyll, and after attaining dramatic scenes, he succumbs. Only after his death is the dual character of his person revealed.

Northshire man is Mr. Barrymore's leading woman and the picture, which is a Paramount Artery, was directed by John S. Robertson.

THE SCOTTS

Richard Scott and wife of the Con. T. Kennedy shows spent two days in Chicago last week in a visit to his aunt, Mrs. Annie Debardlebien, of 6540 Grove avenue. Mr. Scott has been with the Con. T. Kennedy shows for more than a year a great responsibility with that company. He has absolute charge of the sleeping and dining of this vast aggregation, having the sole management of nine sleeping cars, one private car and the dining car. He has nine assistants and cares for 450 people. His position is unprecedented for a member of the team, the responsibilities of which a nature that it would be almost impossible to replace him in the person of one individual. For instance, he has charge and operates the motors which generate the electricity for the eleven cars, he lets out contracts for laundry, cloths pressing, etc.; he collects all monies for births, meals, etc., pays out bills presented, has the living discharged, does the living wife, Mrs. Scott, does the catering for the private car, in which travel the owner of the shows and his family. The Con. T. Kennedy shows appeared all of the past week at Elgin, and proceeds from that place to Freeport. Ill. Mr. Scott's season with this company is for thirty weeks during the year; the balance of the work is done by the partner of Prof. Means in the Prince of India Company.

"LOCKED LIPS"

At the States Theater next Sunday theater-goers of this city will be given an opportunity to see the Japanese Bernhardt, Tauri Aold, in her latest photodrama, "Locked Lips." The success scored by Miss Aold in her first Universal production, "The Breath of the Gods," has established this Japanese actress as one of the foremost figures on the screen. "Locked Lips" differs from any other photodrama not produced in which a Japanese character holds the center of the screen. Its scenes are not laid out plainly. There is no theme nor situation that is foreign to an American audience. The story opens in a clubroom in an American city where Henry Stanwood, in a brawl over a game of cards, shoots a man and makes his escape. Fleeing from justice, his wanderers bring him to the island of Hilo. There he breaks into a cottage in search of food. It is home of Lotus Blossom, a Japanese girl who touched a native school. She nurses him back to health, strength and they are eventually married. The man's real nature soon asserts itself and he deserts Lotus Blossom. His escape from the island, the changing of clothes with a body which had been washed ashore, his marriage to a girl in San Francisco and Blossom's real identity interrupted when she finds that her husband is married to the woman who had befriended her, are told in a series of gripping, tense situations that end in a breathless olimax. The production shows the directorial of William C. Dowlan, while the star is requested by such players as Stanhope Wheateroff, Maigda Lane, Jack Abbey and others.

"DR. JEKYLL AND MR. HYDE"

Robert Louis Stevenson's powerful story, "Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde," has been translated to the screen with John Barrymore in the leading role and will be shown at the Vendome theater for three days, commencing on Thursday of the present week. This is declared the most remarkable pictures over filmed, and the performance of Mr. Barrymore, it is claimed, is a worthy companion to that of Richard Mansfield, appeared in the same part in the stage play of Lord Macaulay. The plot, as is well known, has its central figure a young London physician, Dr. Jekyll, whose tender-heartedness is exceeded only by his all-absorbing interest in his chosen profession. He is an esteemed inocent beautiful girl, Millicent Curew. Her father, Sir George Curew, is a famous man about town. Dr. Jekyll is persuaded by Sir George to accompany him to a low musical hall. From his study of his scientific investigations, he is appointed with a liquid that will separate the good and evil in a man into two distinct bodies. He evolves such a drug, administers it to himself and becomes not only Jekyll, the doctor-shaped, but also Hyde, a misshapen, villian god. Gradually, side of his character overmasters Jekyll, and after attaining dramatic scenes, he succumbs. Only after his death is the dual character of his person revealed. Northshire man is Mr. Barrymore's leading woman and the picture, which is a Paramount Artery, was directed by John S. Robertson.

THE SCOTTS

Richard Scott and wife of the Con. T. Kennedy shows spent two days in Chicago last week in a visit to his aunt, Mrs. Annie Debardlebien, of 6540 Grove avenue. Mr. Scott has been with the Con. T. Kennedy shows for more than a year a great responsibility with that company. He has absolute charge of the sleeping and dining of this vast aggregation, having the sole management of nine sleeping cars, one private car and the dining car. He has nine assistants and cares for 450 people. His position is unprecedented for a member of the team, the responsibilities of which a nature that it would be almost impossible to replace him in the person of one individual. For instance, he has charge and operates the motors which generate the electricity for the eleven cars, he lets out contracts for laundry, cloths pressing, etc.; he collects all monies for births, meals, etc., pays out bills presented, has the living discharged, does the living wife, Mrs. Scott, does the catering for the private car, in which travel the owner of the shows and his family. The Con. T. Kennedy shows appeared all of the past week at Elgin, and proceeds from that place to Freeport. Ill. Mr. Scott's season with this company is for thirty weeks during the year; the balance of the work is done by the partner of Prof. Means in the Prince of India Company.
