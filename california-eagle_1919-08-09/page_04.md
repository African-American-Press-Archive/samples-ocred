J. B. BASS.....Editor

The Odd Fellows have been in session during this week and have demonstrated that this organization is to the forefront in the march of progress and the people appreciate this grand and noble order to the utmost.

We appreciate the fact that Law and Order seems to be in evidence in our city, and when these two agencies perform their functions, we are on the eve of greater and better days.

Two officials must be given high credit for their efficiency and their zealous performance of their duties and those two officials are none other than Chief of Police Home and District Attorney Thomas Lee Woolwine. Just look at the accomplishment in the Lawler bomb case.

The Progressive Business Men's League is going to the front and demonstrating that their work is a mighty force for future progress along the line to business effort.

The Good Book has said, "He hearth in secret and rewarded openly," but in the case of the church next door to the Eagle, we think the Good Lord will have to change his tactics.

The Los Angeles Express, as should every American Newspaper, takes stand against lynch law:

"A letter lying on my desk advocates lynch law for bomb throwers, no doubt because the writer believes that lynch law will cure the evil.

"A newspaper writer suffering from the same reaction of horror that sent a shudder through the whole city advocates some peculiar and special form of punishment of such friends as the one who tried to burn the Lawler family in their beds.

Probably it will surprise and pain these good citizens—for they are good citizens intent on making life secure—to be told that lynch law and torture are but other facets of the passion that caused the Lawler tragedy. They are manifestations of a hate.

"The most tremendous problem that faces humanity today is the destruction of hate in ALL its manifestations. The world will be a shamibles until that task is accomplished. We cannot accomplish it by ourselves committing violence. The war should have taught us that if it taught us nothing else.

## AMERICAN PREJUDICE.

Prior to the world war, American prejudice in the true sense was a closed book. Of course there were newspaper sensations from time to time, but always the Negro was painted up in such bright colors as pertaining to his brutality and passion for white women that the peoples of Europe concluded that the American Negro was a dangerous character.

It is said that when Negro soldiers first went into certain parts of France the population took every precaution of protecting themselves against the terrible blacks, but it is said, by Negro soldiers who passed through this man testing travail of the soul that they remained in France long enough to prove that they possessed greater manhood qualities than their perpetrators.

Teaching English Race Riots (Chicago Deserters)

London, England, June 20. In order to demonstrate that event country's attitude is the same toward the man with dark skin a group of white men

from the United States started a race riot here last week. More than a thousand British subjects, who were misled by the whites are locked in the brideswell at Liverpool awaiting trial. A method of propaganda has been established by Americans in this city and literature seeking to display racial prejudice has been distributed broadcast. The fact that there was no social barrier between the races here so angered the Americans that insulting remarks were hurled at mixed couples as they were strolling down the boulevard. Row Starts in Cafe.

The first bitter feeling became apparent in Cafe De Montricello, where several Americans were having a dinner party. The proprietor of the resort, absolutely free from prejudice, seated several mixed couples adjacent to the dinner table of the whites. Blood arose in the eyes of the Americans and insulting remarks were directed at the couples. The management objected to the uncoath manner of the whites and ordered them from the cafe. On leaving one of the members slyly pulled the cloth from the table occupied by the couples. This action caused a rough and tumble fight in which the British were victorious.

## Whites Rob Taxi.

Matters remained quiet until the fall of night. Under cover of darkness the whites held up a taxicab and robbed two Britishers of 300 Pounds. Following this they entered the homes of the black population during their absence and wrecked furniture, carried it into the streets and fired it. When this news was spread the British and Americans engaged in fierce fights.

## British Speak Out.

Since the Americans have been allowed to enter fashionable clubs here that have no rules relating to color, they have endeavored to impress the British with the ract that such a rule is necessary to preserve the superiority of the white race. In the attempt to avoid clashes several cafe owners have ordered that the American whites be dented the privilege to eat in the main dining rooms. In an interview with a reporter Sir Albertus Markham said: "In observing the cause and effect of racial troubles it can be traced directly to the influence of the propaganda put in operation by the Americans during their sojourn in our midst as fighter of democracy. I can emphatically say that England knew no race troubles prior to the advent of the American soldiers. Our conception of what democracy really means does not carry with it race discrimination and burning of human beings. England has long since relegated that idea to the background and she cannot afford at this late day to mar her brilliant record."

Parliament has been asked to investigate the cause of the trouble arising between the races for the past few weeks.

MES. ERNEST JONES ENTERTAINS HER DAUGHTER ERNESTINE UPON THE OCCASION OF HER 13TH BIRTHDAY.

Unique in every way was the birthday lunchcon given by Mrs. Jones in honor of her little daughter Ernestine's birthday.

Ernestine is a bright little Miss, a recent graduate of the 14th St. Grammar School and one of the star pupils of the Wilkin's School of Musis.

The luncheon was a pleasant surprise to Ernestine. Of course mother had never forgotten her jon this occasion, but just what was going to happen this time little Miss Ernestine could not guess. However, at high noon when she answered the door bell, and was met there by two of her little friends, who showered her with tokens of love and appreciation accompanied by kind words she realized that her mother though in many ways how to make her succeeding birthdays happier than the previous ones.

Her delight was kindled into overflowing joy when she peeped into the dining room and saw there a table loaded with good things, and charmingly decorated.

The color scheme of pink and white was modestly but prettily carried out. In the center of the table was one big blushing Cupy who held a white ribbon that extended to the five places set and swinging on the end of each ribbon was a miniature Cupy with dilated lids watched over his place.

The guests were the Misses Georgia Caruthers; Charlotte Brown; Maud Madison and Mrs. J. B. Bass.

The little Misses after spending a delightful afternoon departed voting Mrs. Jones mistress or the art of entertaining.
