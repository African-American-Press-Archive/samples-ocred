Rev. William P. Lawrence was born in Caroline County, Va. in 1865. He attended the county schools, going to Hampton and to the Virginia Union University, then the Richmond Theological Seminary. He later took a course at Bethany Bible Institute, Pittsburg, Pa. He was honored with the degree of Doctor of Divinity, as well as that of Doctor of Philosophy. He was the first paid general secretary of the Colored Branch of the Philadelphia Y. M. C. A.

He has held pastorates at Pittsburg, Pa.; Elimira, N. Y.; Syracuse, N. Y.; and Elizabeth, N. J. He was installed as pastor of Union Baptist Church of Orange on the 31st of January, 1906. The sermon on this occasion was preached by Rev. George E. Morris, now of Camden, N. J.

When Rev. Lawrence came to the Union Church he found the church in debt and very much disorganized. It has steadily grown under his guidance and is now one of the largest churches in New Jersey and second to none in importance. As far back as 1608 there has been a desire to build a church away from the noise of trains and mills and so a house and lot were bought at the corner of Oakwood avenue and Wilson place which has since been used as a passageway. The location is ideal and one of the greatest desires of the life of Rev. Lawrence was to see a church effected on the spot.

The officers of Union Baptist Church are as follows. Trustees, A. A. Hill, E. G. Richardson, Charles Smith, C. P. Tinsley, J. R. Thompson, Aaran Woods, and Henry Gordon; deans, Henry Watkins, L. B. Minor, Berry Pittman, Mr. Royd, Mr. Fourmoy and Mr. Aaran Woods. The superintendent of the Sunday School is Mr. L. H Hatley. The choir master is Mr. J. Howard Buford, and the church clerk is T. W. Middleworth.

Rev. Lawrence was a progressive man and interested in everything in the community and State that stood for advancement. He was president of the Progressive Building and Loyal Association, president of the Interdenominal Ministers' Union of the Oranges, president of the American Sunday School Convention of New Jersey, a member of Oral Lodge, Grand United Order of Old Fellows, and of Celestial Council No. 16. Independent Order of St Lukes, he was a firm believer in the work of the Oakwood Avenue Baptist Branch of the Y. M. C. A. and of the East Orange Social Settlement he was a frequent visitor at both these institutions. He looked for warmly to the holding of the Union Fair, for the Orange Memorial Hospital and often spoke of the deet of gratitude due that hospital by his people. He was beloved by all in the community regardless of race. He was a member of the Executive Committee of the Orange Branch of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People.

He was married to Miss Jessie C Brown on October 31st, 1894, in St Mark's W. E. Church, New York City. Rev. Earned Lynns performed the ceremony. Mrs. Lawrence who survived him, has been an able helper in all the work of his life. He leaves two nieces and two brothers.

(By Charles Edward Russell.)

(By Charles Edward Russell.)

When, before the War, Wendel Phillips denounced chattel slavery, he was assailed by the slaveholding interests of the South; when, after the war, he rejected all proffers of high political office and threatened wage-slavery, he was assailed by the manufacturing interests of the North. In both instances he threatened somebody's profits. That was all, and for this reason Southern fire-eaters offered a price for his head.

Yet, in his long life of ceaseless activities, he debated for no crown, argued for no fees, strove for no reward, sought no place nor any fame, cared for no achievement for its own sake, and used his unequalled gifts only for some cause of justice or freedom, in which he could earn nothing but obliquity, hatred and isolation.

No man over gave up more for the sake of his faith. All his brilliant career was wrecked in all instant. His friends and family deserted him. Some of his relatives declared that he was in the midst and planned to have him confined in an asylum. The press covered him with rabble and abuse. For more than twenty years lived in daily danger of his life, with a price on his head.

For more than twenty years he lived in daily danger of his life with a price on his head.

When a cause was won, as in the cases of the abolition of slavery, in the South, and in the natural revival of popular feeling men sought to make him its hero, he put aside their tributes and demanded their attention to the next unpopular reform. Compared with such a career, the stories of the men that on the grounds of material triumph have won place in the world's regard, seem poor indeed. They toiled for themselves, or for the glory of achievement.

This man's single and unselfish purpose was to win better conditions for the fortunate, wherever they might be, to strive against injustice to further brotherhood, to spread liberty.

As ardently as other men sought wealth and power, he sought the Common Good.

He attended the county schools, going to Hampton and to the Virginia Union University, then the Richmond Theological Seminary. He later took a course at Bethany Bible Institute, Pittsburg, Pa. He was honored with the degree of Doctor of Divinity, as well as that of Doctor of Philosophy. He was the first paid general secretary of the Colored Branch of the Philadelphia Y. M. C. A. He has held pastorates at Pittsburg, Pa.; Elimira, N. Y.; Syracuse, N. Y.; and Elizabeth, N. J. He was installed as pastor of Union Baptist Church of Orange on the 31st of January, 1906. It has steadily grown under his guidance and is now one of the largest churches in New Jersey and second to none in importance. (By Charles Edward Russell.) When, before the War, Wendel Phillips denounced chattel slavery, he was assailed by the slaveholding interests of the South; when, after the war, he rejected all proffers of high political office and threatened wage-slavery, he was assailed by the manufacturing interests of the North. In both instances he threatened somebody's profits. That was all, and for this reason Southern fire-eaters offered a price for his head. Yet, in his long life of ceaseless activities, he debated for no crown, argued for no fees, strove for no reward, sought no place nor any fame, cared for no achievement for its own sake, and used his unequalled gifts only for some cause of justice or freedom, in which he could earn nothing but obliquity, hatred and isolation. No man over gave up more for the sake of his faith. All his brilliant career was wrecked in all instant. His friends and family deserted him. Some of his relatives declared that he was in the midst and planned to have him confined in an asylum. The press covered him with rabble and abuse. For more than twenty years lived in daily danger of his life, with a price on his head.

For more than twenty years he lived in daily danger of his life with a price on his head. When a cause was won, as in the cases of the abolition of slavery, in the South, and in the natural revival of popular feeling men sought to make him its hero, he put aside their tributes and demanded their attention to the next unpopular reform. Compared with such a career, the stories of the men that on the grounds of material triumph have won place in the world's regard, seem poor indeed. They toiled for themselves, or for the glory of achievement. This man's single and unselfish purpose was to win better conditions for the fortunate, wherever they might be, to strive against injustice to further brotherhood, to spread liberty. As ardently as other men sought wealth and power, he sought the Common Good.
