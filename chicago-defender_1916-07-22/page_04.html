RACE ACTORS

The Vittagraph Blue Ribbon feature "Kennedy Square," which comes to The States theatre on Sunday, July 23, has among the members of the cast several members of the Race. The picture is a remarkable production and is devoid of any action that savors of the low comedy generally used where Race performers are used. Indeed, one of the principal characters is acted by a member of the Race and the writer reviewed the feature before it was hooked, to insure against any objectionable features.

Antonio Moreno, the celebrated star, heads a great cast in this production. The story tells of St. George Temple, an aristocratic old gentleman, who lives in his fine home in Kennedy Square, and who is liked by everyone. Harry Rutter tells him of his love affair and his troubles and the old gentleman acts as peacemaker between Rutter and Kate, daughter of Hon. Douglas Seymour. It proves a thankless job, and after a fight and a duel in the Rutter home between Harry and a rival sultor, Harry is put out of the house by his father and comes to live with St. George. Things go from bad to worse, when St. George loses his money and Harry goes away to South America. After making a fortune from a ruby mine, the young man returns to find the old gentleman living in poverty, and makes full restitution. This time St. George acts the part of peacemaker successfully and Kate and Harry are married.

The piece is in five great parts and is full of action and heart's interest. The Sunday matinee begins at 2:30.

CLUBS and SOCIETIES

The Mystic Social Club, No. 1, largely attended the entertainment afforded them by Mrs. Jennie Jones, 3414 South State street, Thursday, July 13. Instrumental and vocal renditions from the latest compositions were most eulogistic and these, together with the fine repast and general good time, form common features on evening spent with this sociable hostess. Mrs. Jessie Smith, president; L. Smith, editor.

The Wednesday Afternoon Art Club met last week with Mrs. Hattie Jenkins, 3542 Vernon avenue. Mrs. Minnie Mapp of Los Angeles, Cal., was a visitor. The ladies spent a very pleasant afternoon. Mrs. Ella Washington, president; Mrs. Mabel Powell, edirress.

Chicago Union Charity Club met July 13 at 4236 Wabash avenue. Mrs. Essie Carey, the hostess, served a dainty repast. Next meeting July 27 with Mrs. Barber, 3516 Forest avenue. Mrs. Lucy Livingston, president; Mrs. Hattie Jenkins, corresponding secretary; Mrs. Corrina Woodson, reporter.

The Beryle Pleasure Club was entertained by Mr. and Mrs. Steve Matson July 17. Refreshments were served. Next meeting July 31 with Mr. and Mrs. Russell Kemp, 5727 Wabash avenue. George R. Woodson, president; Walter E. Sheridan, secretary.

The Ideal Art Embroidery Club met July 13 at the residence of Mrs. Pemberton, 257 East 45th street. After the routine of business, the club was delightfully entertained by the president, Mrs. Pemberton. Visitors present, three. Mrs. Clara Brown, reporter; Mrs. Pemberton, president.

The Clover Leaf Social Club met at the residence of Mrs. F. Woodson last Thursday. The arrangements for the annual outing were completed and an enjoyable afternoon was spent and a good number were present despite the torrid heat. Mrs. M. Blackburn, president; Mrs. A. Grayson, secretary.

The Progressive Negro League held a fine meeting and much enthusiasm was created by the different members. Several visitors were present and spoke encouragingly concerning the work performed by the league. Among them were Misses White and Bell, teachers in Kansas City, now attending the Chicago University. The principal speaker was Rev. B. U. Taylor, pastor of St. Stephens A. M. E. Church of the West Side, whose address was heartily received and much appreciated by all present. Those who took part in the program were Mrs. Taylor and Messrs. Harvey Turner, Wallace, Bailey and Tompkins. It was announced that on the first Sunday in August, Major John R. Lynch would address the league on "His Experience and Travels in Cuba." The league adjourned to meet Wednesday evening at the residence of Mr. Edgar Tompkins, 5460 Dearborn street.

The Royal Art Club met with Mrs. Birdie Tillford, Wednesday, July 12th at 3558 Prairie avenue. Had a very nice attendance and a pleasant afternoon was spent in work. Refreshments were served, which all enjoyed. Mrs. Sarah Taylor will entertain the club in Washington Park, July 13th, if weather permits. E. Drakeford, president; N. Hall, secretary.

The Lincoln Saving League met at the home of Mrs. Nellie Maxwell, 3132 Dearborn street, Monday, July 17. Old-fashioned barbecue meat was served. Next meeting will be held July 24 at the residence of Mrs. Nannie Smith. Mrs. Alice Vance, president; Mrs. A. B. Forrest, secretary.

At Douglas Center Sunday, July 23 at 4 p. m., Mrs. Cella Parker Woolley will review a popular play, "A Bent Twig," setting forth home life and influence. Miss Mabel Wagner will give a group of piano selections. Friday evening, July 28, at 8 p. m., will occur the next students' social with address by some noted speaker and music in charge of Prof. James Mundy, one of our most noted musicians.

At Wendell Phillips Settlement the summer is being well spent, the Boys' Club meeting Tuesday evenings, the Camp Fire Girls Wednesday at 3:30 p. m. and Story Hour Thursday at 9:30 p. m., the girls being in charge of Miss Jessie Jones, a much appreciated worker. The head resident, Mrs. A. C. Cone, is busy with outside lectures, receptions and soon to entertain Mrs. Booker T. Washington with social dinner.

The Y. W. E. Club are so very much grieved to give up their faithful member, Mrs. Sarrah Herring, but we hope to meet her in the sweet by and by, where parting will be over.

The Negro Fellowship league conference Sunday, July 23, on "Labor Unions and What They Mean to the Negro." Discussion led by Mr. William Hannon. Meeting promptly at 4 o'clock at the reading room, 3005 State street.

Last Sunday Sargeant Hightower gave a most interesting address on "Political Conditions in the Second Ward." His address was discussed by Messrs. George H. Jackson, L. W. Washington and others. Mr. J. E. Hughes' Race review was discussed; It is becoming more interesting every week. Mrs. Ida B. Wells Barnett, president.

The Alpha Suffrage club held a most successful lawn, fete on the spacious grounds of the Y. W. C. A. Wednesday evening, July 19. Next meeting of the club will be a business one, the first Wednesday in August, at the reading room, 3005 State street.

MRS. LOUISE HOWARD DIES
Las Vegas, N. M., July 21—Mrs. Louise Howard, wife of Sam Howard, formerly of Chicago, Ill., died here last week and was buried Sunday from Brown's A. M. E.-Chapel. Her husband is a member of Great Lakes Lodge; Chicago.

Reply—Dear Doc: In reply, will say that your question is a deep one, also you should have found out if the friend of a friend of yours was a friend of mine. Mosquitoes, at a temperature of 60 degrees or less catch cold, which makes them hourse. This temperature is always reached about Oct. 32nd. While they bore as well after that date as during the summer season, they do their work in silence, thus, especially in Mississippi, getting much of their dirty work blamed upon your other friend, the clichen, whom you generally have with you the year around.—Editor.

THIS HAPPENED!

Birmingham. Ala.—Something happened here at the Savoy theater a few nights ago. The Savoy cuters to the better class of Race people and does an excellent business. It is under the management of Messrs. Greener & Tampert. The theater was well filled when a "white" man walked in with his hat on. Mr. Greener, who was standing near the entrance, ordered him to remove his hat as a gentleman should.

"I am looking for a boy," said the cracker, "and, too, I'm a gentleman."

"Well, you are not acting like one," said Mr. Greener. "These ladies and gentlemen must be respected. I am running this place, and your hat comes off or out you go. And that means right now!" reaching for the man's hat. Some sharp words were exchanged as the man left the theater. He was standing outside making insulting remarks about "niggers" and about how much respect they were entitled to from a "white" gentleman when Mr. Greener came out and landed on the beast's jaw. He went down in a heap on the sidewalk. Then arose and "heat it" up the street, vowing all kinds of vengeance. The protection given the patrons of the Savoy against rowdyism of all sorts has made it the most popular place of amusement in Birmingham.

THIS HAPPENED!

Birmingham. Ala.—Something happened here at the Savoy theater a few nights ago. The Savoy cuters to the better class of Race people and does an excellent business. It is under the management of Messrs. Greener & Tampert. The theater was well filled when a "white" man walked in with his hat on. Mr. Greener, who was standing near the entrance, ordered him to remove his hat as a gentleman should. "I am looking for a boy," said the cracker, "and, too, I'm a gentleman." "Well, you are not acting like one," said Mr. Greener. "These ladies and gentlemen must be respected. I am running this place, and your hat comes off or out you go. And that means right now!" reaching for the man's hat. Some sharp words were exchanged as the man left the theater. He was standing outside making insulting remarks about "niggers" and about how much respect they were entitled to from a "white" gentleman when Mr. Greener came out and landed on the beast's jaw. He went down in a heap on the sidewalk. Then arose and "heat it" up the street, vowing all kinds of vengeance. The protection given the patrons of the Savoy against rowdyism of all sorts has made it the most popular place of amusement in Birmingham.
