## RACE ACTORS

The Vittagraph Blue Ribbon feature "Kennedy Square," which comes to The States theatre on Sunday, July 23, has among the members of the cast several members of the Race. The picture is a remarkable production and is devoid of any action that savors of the low comedy generally used where Race performers are used. Indeed, one of the principal characters is acted by a member of the Race and the writer reviewed the feature before it was hooked, to insure against any objectionable features.

Antonio Moreno, the celebrated star, heads a great cast in this production. The story tells of St. George Temple, an aristocratic old gentleman, who lives in his fine home in Kennedy Square, and who is liked by everyone. Harry Rutter tells him of his love affair and his troubles and the old gentleman acts as peacemaker between Rutter and Kate, daughter of Hon. Douglas Seymour. It proves a thankless job, and after a fight and a duel in the Rutter home between Harry and a rival sultor, Harry is put out of the house by his father and comes to live with St. George. Things go from bad to worse, when St. George loses his money and Harry goes away to South America. After making a fortune from a ruby mine, the young man returns to find the old gentleman living in poverty, and makes full restitution. This time St. George acts the part of peacemaker successfully and Kate and Harry are married.

The piece is in five great parts and is full of action and heart's interest. The Sunday matinee begins at 2:30.

## CLUBS and SOCIETIES

The Mystic Social Club, No. 1, largely attended the entertainment afforded them by Mrs. Jennie Jones, 3414 South State street, Thursday, July 13. Instrumental and vocal renditions from the latest compositions were most eulogistic and these, together with the fine repast and general good time, form common features on evening spent with this sociable hostess. Mrs. Jessie Smith, president; L. Smith, editor.

The Wednesday Afternoon Art Club met last week with Mrs. Hattie Jenkins, 3542 Vernon avenue. Mrs. Minnie Mapp of Los Angeles, Cal., was a visitor. The ladies spent a very pleasant afternoon. Mrs. Ella Washington, president; Mrs. Mabel Powell, edirress.

Chicago Union Charity Club met July 13 at 4236 Wabash avenue. Mrs. Essie Carey, the hostess, served a dainty repast. Next meeting July 27 with Mrs. Barber, 3516 Forest avenue. Mrs. Lucy Livingston, president; Mrs. Hattie Jenkins, corresponding secretary; Mrs. Corrina Woodson, reporter.

The Beryle Pleasure Club was entertained by Mr. and Mrs. Steve Matson July 17. Refreshments were served. Next meeting July 31 with Mr. and Mrs. Russell Kemp, 5727 Wabash avenue. George R. Woodson, president; Walter E. Sheridan, secretary.

The Ideal Art Embroidery Club met July 13 at the residence of Mrs. Pemberton, 257 East 45th street. After the routine of business, the club was delightfully entertained by the president, Mrs. Pemberton. Visitors present, three. Mrs. Clara Brown, reporter; Mrs. Pemberton, president.

The Clover Leaf Social Club met at the residence of Mrs. F. Woodson last Thursday. The arrangements for the annual outing were completed and an enjoyable afternoon was spent and a good number were present despite the torrid heat. Mrs. M. Blackburn, president; Mrs. A. Grayson, secretary.

# THIS HAPPENED!

Birmingham. Ala.—Something happened here at the Savoy theater a few nights ago. The Savoy cuters to the better class of Race people and does an excellent business. It is under the management of Messrs. Greener & Tampert. The theater was well filled when a "white" man walked in with his hat on. Mr. Greener, who was standing near the entrance, ordered him to remove his hat as a gentleman should.

"I am looking for a boy," said the cracker, "and, too, I'm a gentleman."

"Well, you are not acting like one," said Mr. Greener. "These ladies and gentlemen must be respected. I am running this place, and your hat comes off or out you go. And that means right now!" reaching for the man's hat. Some sharp words were exchanged as the man left the theater. He was standing outside making insulting remarks about "niggers" and about how much respect they were entitled to from a "white" gentleman when Mr. Greener came out and landed on the beast's jaw. He went down in a heap on the sidewalk. Then arose and "heat it" up the street, vowing all kinds of vengeance. The protection given the patrons of the Savoy against rowdyism of all sorts has made it the most popular place of amusement in Birmingham.
