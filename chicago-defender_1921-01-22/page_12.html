THE LABOR MUDDLE

THE LABOR SITUATION is claiming the attention of all thoughtful Americans at this time, for it is felt that unless radical steps are immediately taken to check immigration the flood of cheap help will completely demoralize the field and cause that great body of men enrolled as members of the American Federation of Labor to wage a relentless war not only against the intruders, but against employers and those responsible for their admission to our land. It is not held that the foreigner should not, if he so desires, make his home with us; but to prevent him from being a liability rather than an asset, we should reserve the right to limit the number of entries and to place them in sections of the country where their services are most needed.

SUCH A RULING might be a bit undemocratic, but pardonable when it is realized, by its enforcement, will come the greatest good to the greatest number. From the rural sections of the North, South and West comes the cry for tillers of the soil, herders of cattle, choppers of wood, but the cry falls on deaf ears. The dearth of American workmen in these fields is due primarily to the late war. Farmers' sons returning from battle found city life and city wages more attractive. The case of the immigrant is much the same.

NEARLY ONE AND A HALF MILLION IMMIGRANTS landed on our shores in twelve months. A few of the eastern states got half of that number; 90 per cent of this half, it is estimated, settled in New York and Boston, just where they were not wanted and where they complicated the labor and industrial situation pitifully. Another large group of them settled in Pittsburgh and Chicago. The portion that went to the land—the rural districts—was almost negligible. Out of all this vast number only 21,346 found their way to eleven Southern states—Virginia, North and South Carolina, Georgia, Florida, Alabama, Tennessee, Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas and Maryland—states that need thousands to carry on the work. The West got but a handful, the North less than that.

FROM A PURELY SELFISH VIEWPOINT we could hold the immigrant as a menace. Surely we are the greatest sufferers by his presence, for with the American white man's prejudice to combat, no matter how efficient we have proven to be, when the question of choosing between 100 per cent American black labor and ignorant and oftimes traitorous, foreign white labor arises we are invariably thrown into the discard, there to await another emergency, there to be held by unscrupulous employers as a club over union labor, while these that speak a different tongue, have different customs, who know nothing of the laws of the land and care less, are put on the pay roll. By dint of hard work and expressions of friendly feeling the unionized labor forces of America let down their bars in most branches and admitted us to membership.

CHICAGO'S TRACTION PROBLEM

MAYOR THOMPSON'S personal popularity in the state of Illinois and especially in Chicago is due largely to the fact that the people believe that the mayor is due in his fight for a 5 cent car fare. The city has a contract with the street car company with several years yet to run. In spite of that contract, Governor Lowden's utility mission ignored the same and increased the rate to 8 cents on the surface and 12 cents on the elevated lines. Mayor Thompson's administration has confronted against these increases. In the fight the masses of the people are with him.

THE FACT has been brought out by his investigators, chiefly by Engineer Jackson, that a 5 cent fare will yield sufficient revenue to meet all legitimate demands on the company, including a handsome dividend to the stockholders and that the city will employ the employees. The city administration has a plan looking to the municipal government. The city car system, which no doubt is feasible and practical, yet several years must necessarily intervene before the same can be put into effect. From this palpable injustice put upon the traveling public by the Lowden utility company, a proposed especially by the city council that Governor Small, whose recall and election were due largely to the unpopularity of the acts of the Lowden utility commission, appoint a new commission and that this body immediately formed a new predecessors. It is contended by some that this cannot be legally done, and that the enforcement of the same would be immediately enjoined. If this be true the people will be no worse off than they are now and the experiment is well proven that the commission could not be enjoined in setting aside the contract and increasing the commission to propose that a subsequent ruling abrogating that decision would not be enjoined.

IN ORDER that we may do no man an injustice we admit that here and there can be found union men who believe there should be no distinction in membership based on the color of one's skin, but the thinkers have shown the great majority the handwriting on the wall and advised as a mere matter of self-protection to admit the black workmen. Ours has been an interesting fight; enemies to the left of us, enemies to the right of us, but fortunately the road clear ahead of us, and that road we have followed until it has brought us to the point where we are an important factor in the economic work. To hold this place we must join hands with the American laborers as a whole to petition the powers that be to close our ports against immigration until such a time as conditions here warrant their opening.

WE ARE STRONGLY IN FAVOR of municipal ownership on general principle and hope this will be the final outcome. The people as a whole are confident that we will be able to make this happen through commissioners or trustees of their own selection; than through the medium of a private corporation. These commissioners or trustees being involved in the municipal official news, all of our citizens would have a voice in this as well as in other boroughs, and could be made free from any restriction or limitation of any kind, whatsoever.

STEAMSHIP OFFICIALS' admit that their lines have been solidly booked for steerage passengers from Europe for over a year ahead. It has been estimated that nearly 5,000,000 are waiting to pay us a visit. We repeat if the immigrants could be properly directed and compelled to settle directly in the country where their labor is really needed, rather than to hang around large cities, no objection would be made to their entrance. When cities grow at the rate of 25 per cent and the country at 3 per cent something is going to happen, and soon. We have thousands of abandoned farms. No labor to work them. Agriculture is our biggest business and we are letting it get strangled to death. The situation is becoming desperate. Let us be charitable, but let us also remember that self protection is the first law of nature.

SECRETARY OF STATE

REPORTS FROM MARION indicate that Charles E. Hughes of New York has been selected as Secretary of State. No better choice could have been made. As governor of New York and as a justice of the United States Supreme Court he has demonstrated to the satisfaction of the public that he is a strong advocate for the suppression of our vote at the South, he instead of Wilson would now be President, because it was through this means his defeat in 1916 was accomplished.

OR, SINCE it is a sort of national custom for an acceptable man to be given a second term the probabilities are that instead of being the retiring president he would succeed himself. So far as we are concerned we find nothing in his predecessor's unfavorable comment. His selection indicates the determination of Senator Harding to surround himself with the best, strongest and most influential cabinet ministers obtainable.

PROMINENT bankers, merchants and manufacturers see good times ahead, and undoubtedly if we all participate in the will come. In the meantime, however, keep putting that extra change in your savings bank, spending only for the necessities. Get the luxuries in flush times.

IT MUST BE ADMITTED that the United States is exceedingly moist for a dry country. True, the saloons are closed and the distilleries are no longer producing the liquids that intoxicate, but the thirst have little difficulty in appeasing them. Their one card that "such a law restricts a man's rights and privileges" is trumped by the query, what of-the-innocents that suffer through the drinker's indiscretions? According to the testimony of a number of wives their husbands are now better, more prosperous and more impossible for them to get the liquor, but because it costs more and even then they are not sure of what they are getting, consequently they do without it.

IT HAS BEEN NOTED that very few convicts are breaking prison these days. Perhaps it is too hard to make a living outside.

IF SANTA CLAUS didn't bring you what you wanted, don't blame us—we told him to.

THE POLICE may not be making progress in the Hyde Park bombing mysteries, but at least they are not losing any of the information they have had. They are still sure that there were explosions.

THE ENFORCEMENT of this law, like all others, depends upon the potency of popular sentiment. In localities where it is popular it will be stricter than in the least populous. The least tacitty in its enforcement. In nearly all the larger cities places that were drinking saloons prior to prohibition are now drug stores. In many a thriving business is going on dispensing—in a limited degree—the same sort of drugs as in the other cities. Descriptions issued by physicians. It is difficult to draw the line with reference to the discretion of the physician in prescribing for discusses real or imaginary.

A REMEDY for abuses along this line ought to be effective in all localities. The same sentence that secured the ratification of the eleventh amendment and the law for the enforcement of the same.

EVERY EFFORT is being made to make the Chicago Surface Lines reduce the number of car fare to 5 per mile. Mayor Thomas M. McCarthy has control of city, county and state policies there is every reason to believe this will be brought about. Besides the reduction in fare we are also interested in finding out why this company persistently refuses to give employment to our men. The number of cities are citizens, taxpayers and large numbers of their convoyances. Why do we give it more priority than the day is not far distant when this public utility will be owned and operated by the city. Then we will get our inning.

IT IS PLANE NOW why bull fighting holds its following in Spain. The bull keeps the game honest. Baseball players and managers please note.

SYMPTOMS OF PNEUMONIA
As pneumonia is a very, inadmissible disease, we deem it, wise that, you should thoroughly acquaint yourself with the ordinary symptoms of, this death-dealing disease. Ordinally, we

speak of pneumonia as being a class of classes. The more severe type is known in medical parlance as lobar pneumonia. This is the most common type of pneumonia that occurs in young. It can also run and runs a short but nevertheless severe-course, terminating either in from 3 to 11 days. This type of

pneumonia comes on with a severe chill, rapid, bounding pulse, pain, or stiffness, the skin beats, there is shortness of breath, and, in fact, the respiratory movement of the affected side is rather shallow, painful and accompanied by a throat pain. The neck on the side affected may be flushed, the eyes bright and anxious. There is always fever, running all the way from 100 up to 104 degrees, the skintightness of the son is inclined to be more, at ease or best better lying on the affected side.
