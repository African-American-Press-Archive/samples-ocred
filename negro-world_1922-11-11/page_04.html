THE PASSING OF LYMAN ABBOTT

ON October 21st Dr. Lyman Abbott, former editor-in-chief of the Outlook and successor of Henry Ward Beecher as pastor of the Plymouth Congregational Church, Brooklyn, died in the latter city in his eighty-seventh year. He has not been in the limelight during the past ten years, but in the years between 1890 and 1905 his name was a household word in America.

He was a striking representative of an important phase of American thought. In the late eighties the Darwinian theories of evolution, biblical criticism, the new psychology and the discoveries in astronomy and geology showing that the universe stretched further out in space and reached further back in time than the churchman had been taught to believe created a sensation. Then pious and religious souls began anxiously to inquire whether the new science, psychology and knowledge of the Bible would undermine the faith of the fathers and the eternal foundations of religion.

Then champions of the eternal verities leaped to the fore. Rudolf Lotze, Prof Paulsen, Rudolf Eucken and Prof Otto of Germany, Dr James Martineau, the Seth brothers and the Cairds of England, Dr Wm. T Harris, U S Commissioner of Education, Prof John Watson of Canada, Dean C. C. Everett, Prof William James and Prof Josiah Royce of Harvard, Dr George Trumbull Ladd of Yale, Prof Borden P. Bourne of Boston University and President Jacob Gould Schurman of Cornell University and Prof John Fiske filled the libraries with books that were penetrating, analytical and profound and eloquent in passages showing that modern science had not undermined faith in God, freedom and immortality.

But these works were for the most part too subtle and profound for the undergraduate and for the layman who only had a high school education or a grammar school education supplemented by general reading. Then a group of brilliant preachers came along who acted as the interpreters of the thoughts of the master minds. Dr George A Gordon and Dr C C. Dole of Boston, Dr Theodore Munger and Dr Newman Smythe of New Haven, Coun., Dr Amory Bradford of Montclair and Dr Lyman Abbott of Brooklyn, N. Y., shone brilliantly in this galaxy who distilled the thought of the great scholars and philosophers and gave it to their hearers or the general public in homeopathic doses.

Among this group Dr Lyman Abbott was the most conspicuous. He occupied a strategic position, as he was the successor of the famous Henry Ward Beecher and edited the most popular religious weekly. Then his clear and vigorous style and his faith and enthusiasm, which manifested themselves both in speech and writing, gave him a wide hearing. We remember how the main auditorium and the anterooms of Dwight Hall, Yale University, were packed and jammed one Sunday evening to hear Dr Abbott discourse eloquently for one hour on evolution and religion. Not a great philosopher himself, Dr Abbott was a remarkable popularizer of semi-philosophical thought.

Dr Abbott was also intensely interested in Negro education but while a progressive in religion, he was a reactionary on the race problem. While a radical in theology, he was a conservative when handling the strivings of the black man. He believed in the industrial, but not the higher education of the Negro, and did not think that the time was ripe for the Negro to press his claims to give and political recognition.

We do not think that this was due so much to prejudice per se as to lack of information about the Negro. In a talk with him while he was college pastor at Harvard for a few weeks we found him frank, courteous and cordial, but he had not known intimately Negro men of real scholarship like Dr Alexander Crummell and Dr. Edward Wilmot Blyden, nor had he read of the distinguished foreign Negroes of the eighteenth century. He did not seem to know of the civilizations that black men erected in Egypt, Ethiopia and Timbucoo. Possibly he had heard of these things, but had not taken them seriously or investigated them for himself, and that is perhaps why a semi-philosophical thinker in religion and theological thought, the great divine was only a surface thinker in ethnological thought.

But we must not criticize Dr Abbott too severely. He came upon the scene of action and arrived at manhood's state long before the researches of Sergi, Ripley, Boaz, Emot, Chamberlain, Werner and Major Felix Du Bois revealed the part played by black men in American, medieval and ancient civilization, in an age when Gregoire's Enquiry was not taken seriously in America and in an age when the American translation omitted the splendid tribute to the Negro in Volney's Ruins and the Meditation on the Fate of Ancient Empires.

We must judge men not from the light that we have, but from the light that they had, remembering that they, too, were the product of their environment and were influenced by the Zeitgeist.

Compared with his great predecessor, Henry Ward Becher, Dr Abbott was not as magnetic a preacher, but he was a greater writer. In intellectual things he was a more philosophical thinker, but in things that concern the plain, common, every day man he was not as keen a psychologist as the great Brooklyn preacher, but in the main problem of his times, the problem of theological readjustment to the demands of modern thought, Dr Abbott ably filled his niche.

Centenary Celebration

The celebration of the battle of St. Georges Caye was an entire success on the 11th and 12th of September, 1922. The city was well decorated. Cycle parader motor cars fire engines sociales and the many thousands of school children parading the principal streets of British Honduras in the celebration a mark of history The Americans showed high appreciation with the community. The most attractive feature of the day was the school children, with the American and English flags waving as they passed by, with the strains of the band blending with the voices of the children "Vamos Compadre from St. Georges Caye".

REMAINS OF COL. CHARLES YOUNG, U. S. A., TO BE RETURNED TO THE U. S.

The Colonel Charles Young Post No. 338, American Legion, of New York, has received advices from the War Department relative to the return of the body of the late Colonel, for whom the post has been named.

Colonel Young was military attache to the United States Consul at Monrovia, Liberia. While on a tour of inspection he succumbed to an illness and died at Lagos, Nigeria, last January. Lagos being about 1,500 miles from Monrovia and an English territory, the body, having been buried, was required, according to English rule, to remain six months.

The attached letter received by the post from the War Department will be of interest to the public which has evinced a great interest in the return of this great soldier

October 24. 1922.

To Mr Wm Service Bell, lst Vice Commander, Col Charles Young Post 398, American Legion, 2350 Seventh Avenue, New York, N. Y.
1 Referring to previous correspondence relative to the return to the United States of the remains of the late Colonel Charles Young, you are advised that this office is in receipt of a cable from the American Minister at Monrovia, Liberia, to the effect that authority has been secured of the Nigerian Government for the exhumation of the remains of Colonel Young, also that the Government of Nigeria has consented to take the responsibility of placing the remains on board, and that he is making necessary arrangements with the steamship line at Lagos for transportation of the remains. Inasmuch as the necessary structures have been given relative to the procurement of the casket and details arranged with the Niger Government, it is believed that it be possible to get the remains into country within a very few months.
2 You will be advised fully of the progress made as information is received in this office.
For the Quartermaster General.
H. J. Conner, Assistant.

CASE AGAINST HON. MARCUS
GARVEY SET FOR NOV. 27

The case of the United States vs. Marcus Garvey will be heard in New York on the 27th instant. This case promises to be one of the most interesting in the history of reform movements and their leaders. The world knows that it is not the question of Marcus Garvey, but the ideals of the Universal Negro Improvement Association and the Negro peoples of the world that will be on trial. Negroes everywhere shall organize themselves the more to carry the fight for an emancipated race and a redeemed Africa to the final conclusion. Negro traitors and rival organizations are mainly responsible for the action brought against Mr. Garvey. Mr Garvey, it is stated, will place on the witness stand over a hundred of the plotters who have tried to harass him and the Universal Negro Improvement Association to testify as touching the part in the international conspiracy to incriminate and defend him in the prosecution of the high ideals of Negro Freedom

REMAINS OF COL. CHARLES YOUNG, U. S. A., TO BE RETURNED TO THE U. S.

The Colonel Charles Young Post No. 338, American Legion, of New York, has received advices from the War Department relative to the return of the body of the late Colonel, for whom the post has been named. Colonel Young was military attache to the United States Consul at Monrovia, Liberia. While on a tour of inspection he succumbed to an illness and died at Lagos, Nigeria, last January. Lagos being about 1,500 miles from Monrovia and an English territory, the body, having been buried, was required, according to English rule, to remain six months. The attached letter received by the post from the War Department will be of interest to the public which has evinced a great interest in the return of this great soldier

October 24. 1922.

To Mr Wm Service Bell, lst Vice Commander, Col Charles Young Post 398, American Legion, 2350 Seventh Avenue, New York, N. Y. 1 Referring to previous correspondence relative to the return to the United States of the remains of the late Colonel Charles Young, you are advised that this office is in receipt of a cable from the American Minister at Monrovia, Liberia, to the effect that authority has been secured of the Nigerian Government for the exhumation of the remains of Colonel Young, also that the Government of Nigeria has consented to take the responsibility of placing the remains on board, and that he is making necessary arrangements with the steamship line at Lagos for transportation of the remains. Inasmuch as the necessary structures have been given relative to the procurement of the casket and details arranged with the Niger Government, it is believed that it be possible to get the remains into country within a very few months. 2 You will be advised fully of the progress made as information is received in this office. For the Quartermaster General. H. J. Conner, Assistant.

CASE AGAINST HON. MARCUS GARVEY SET FOR NOV. 27

The case of the United States vs. Marcus Garvey will be heard in New York on the 27th instant. This case promises to be one of the most interesting in the history of reform movements and their leaders. The world knows that it is not the question of Marcus Garvey, but the ideals of the Universal Negro Improvement Association and the Negro peoples of the world that will be on trial. Negroes everywhere shall organize themselves the more to carry the fight for an emancipated race and a redeemed Africa to the final conclusion. Negro traitors and rival organizations are mainly responsible for the action brought against Mr. Garvey. Mr Garvey, it is stated, will place on the witness stand over a hundred of the plotters who have tried to harass him and the Universal Negro Improvement Association to testify as touching the part in the international conspiracy to incriminate and defend him in the prosecution of the high ideals of Negro Freedom
