## Alcohol and Prohibition

From time immemorial people have craved the stimulating effect of alcohol. It seems to me that some of the most brilliant chapters of the world's history were periods when the use of alcohol was universal. Painters, sculptors, poets, men of science and learning, leaders of thought who have added much to the world's advancement have been men who sought the stimulating effects of alcohol.

It serves a legitimate purpose, but, like many other things in this world with which mortal beings come in contact, the weak and undit and inherently susceptible fall prey to its misuse. Reliable statistics state that only about 4% per cent. of the insane and deficient are made so through the use of alcohol.

All men are not susceptible to the ill-effects of alcohol, taken in the proper amounts. Many men who have been daily consumers of alcohol grow ripe in age, forceful in character, robust in intellect, and finally die of some aliment or disease, not due to inflammatory or degenerative changes in the heart, kidney, liver or nervous tissues; and these are the places in the human system which are attacked by alcohol.

On the other hand, we know men whose use of alcohol has not been excessive either in the amount used or the length of time of its ingulgence, who succumb early to a disease brought about by alcoholic indulgence. We see the same results in the use of otic stimulants or foods.

An old saying I have heard—"What is one man's food is another man's poison"—is quite pertinent in the use of alcohol. Individuals may show susceptibilities to certain foods and medicines, percicular to themselves, and not harmful to the majority of other people.

This is due to some inherent weakness or inferiority in the cells of certain of the organs of the body—such as the heart, liver, kidney, nerves or brain.

In fact, the belief of many medical men is that alcoholism is but an inherent inferiority in an individual, and instead of placing the blame on alcohol itself as a poison, the blame should be placed on an inferiority in cell structure and its metabolism or physiological activity; in other words, alcoholism manifests itself only in a physically and mentally abnormal individual, and the majority of the people of this world are not of this class.

This, therefore, is the important point for reformers and anti-prohibition leaders to remember.

The inherently weak, like the poor, are with us all the time, but they are not in the majority; they are the ones who because of this inherent susceptibility and weakness in their constitution, will, by "hook or crook," get a worse substitute for their craving if alcohol of the proper and best kind is prohibited by law. Thus the last state is worse than the first.

The condition today of New York city, and especially of Harlem, with reference to alcoholism is, indeed, bad. ## CHIROPRACTIC AND WHAT IT IS
By: THEO. A. TOBIN. D. PH. C. H.

Chiropractic is a drugless health science of the cause of so-called disease and the art of adjusting the human spine when its segments are out of natural alignment, so that mental impulses can transmit normal health functions to every organ and part of the body.

It is a system of adjusting that includes the use of nothing but the bare hands to locate the vertebra that is out of alignment, and effect its replacement to normal position by a peculiar move, thereby relieving the pressure from the nerve.

The chiropractic theory is that in consequence of mal-position, vertebral segments the openings between the vertebra, through which the spinal nerves pass, are affected, that, in this way the nerves are impaired, and that such compression is the cause of the majority of so-called diseases.

For illustration, a nerve that supplies mental impulses to the stomach is impinged, the carrying capacity of that nerve is decreased, and the mental impulses of life force can not get through the impinged nerve. The result is lack of nerve force to the stomach, and the stomach can not do its function properly and you have stomach disorders. The same is true with other organs in the body.

For illustration, suppose you have a garden and the only means of watering it was by a "water hose." If something fell across the hose what would happen? Of course, the water would stop flowing, and the plants in your garden, not getting the required water, would wither and die. The same thing happens to a nerve that is impinged. To remedy the cause of the obstructed water hose you wouldn't pour oil in the hose or proceed to cut off the hose where the obstruction was, but you would take off the pressure from the hose and the water would then again flow naturally.

That is what the chiropractor does in case of nerve pressure. Nerves demand freedom from pressure to insure good health.

Yesterday was designated as Performance Day—the Sunday nearest October 31 the date on which in 1517 Martin Luther nailed his ninety-five those to the door of Castle Church, Wittenberg—and Lutheran pastors made the German monk the topic of their sermons.

At the Church of the Advent the Rev Augustus Stimle took his text from John 8 36. If the Son shall make you free, he shall be free indeed.

"Authority and freedom the pastor said, 'are the two poles between which the human mind has oscillated since the beginning. They have been favorite subjects with philosophers and the shibboleth of the man in the street in his longing for better times. And man has attempted to spell progress in his increasing repudiation of the one and the eager advocacy of the other.

"Authority has become invested with a hateful ring to the modern car where freedom casts the spell of general music over the heart. Herehow we have not been able to see beyond the contradiction in terms and to find that one supplements the other. We are ever extremists and individualists and Socialists drive us into opposing camps, but the one-sided emphasis of each is really but half of the larger truth that embraces both.

## Luther's Great Service

"The Reformation of the sixteenth century, which we celebrate today was a revolt against authority. Through the stream of the centuries the great truth of which our text speaks has become laid and finally so obscured that man has become a mere cog in the religious machine. It was a form of collective bargaining with heaven that said simply. Join us and we will save you. And when the membership card in the union appeared in the letters of indulgence which Tetel offered the ninety-five theses of Luther ralled to the door of All Saints' Church raised the issue of freedom for the individual soul, and when at Worms he stated his position. Here I stand I cannot do otherwise' modern independence drew its first breath and freedom began once more to pulsate in the arteries of men. "But if the Church of Rome stands for authority as over against freedom, Protestantism suffers from its emphasis on freedom to the utter neglect of authority. We have come upon a time when the danger of a one-sided position is alarmingly evident. "As we look out upon America—and indeed upon the world—today we observe everywhere a temper that is unwilling to submit to any authority. We can easily account for it. An hour her struts on the clock of the world that means a new order of things.

## Liberty Implica Peril

"Liberty always implies peril. It is a test of character. We deplore the destruction of home life, the failure to teach children obedience. In our national life we chronicle race riots, the regard of human life and property mob spirit and a new invasiveness that obtains in so-called best homes. In the industrial world, the most omnious thing is the lack of authority on the part of labor's leaders.

"In the church creeds tend to multiply and denominations to consolidate. The old principle of authority is quite last and it is curious to see the alignment of fundamentalists and liberals the fundamentalists contending with one-sided emphasis for authority and the liberals with similar one-sidedness battling for freedom.

"Freedom of conscience and the authority of Christ were the cardinal points of Luther's teachings. Luther is really the rediscoverer of the individual. It is not true that Luther substituted an infallible book for a fallible Pope. The book must be tested by this touchstone, even Christ. Only so far as it was a record of Christ was it of value to him.

"This year we note the four hundredth anniversary of Luther's translation of the New Testament into the vernacular. He exulted in giving this book to his people because it brought them Christ, and it is the great book of freedom because it brings the authoritative message of the liberator of man."—New York World, Oct. 30.

One of the most enthusiastic meetings witnessed in Harlem for some time was that of the New York Urban League hold at Mother A M. E. Zion Church Sunday afternoon. The church was packed with members and friends of the league.

Arthur C Holden, the new chairman of the Urban League, presided. He was introduced by Miss Elizabeth Walton.

James H Hubert spoke of the accomplishments of the league in New York City, referring especially to the growing interest on the part of the Harlem public and its realization that the league belongs to the people rather than to the Executive Board or those persons who carry on the activities.

Mrs. Mary McLeod Bethune of Dayton, Fin, was the next speaker "All we want is an opportunity. The Negro woman wants the same chance that is given to the white woman. She wants it not as a Negro woman, but as a woman" said Mrs. Bethune Referring to the league's program of interracial cooperation, she declared that no white person need apologize for working with colored people, and no colored person need apologize for working with white people in a program for improving conditions not only of the Negro but our entire land and country.

"For this work of the league," said she, "helps the white man as much as it does the Negro."

Dr John Haynes Holmes said "What has the church to do with the Urban League, and what has the Urban League to do with the church?
