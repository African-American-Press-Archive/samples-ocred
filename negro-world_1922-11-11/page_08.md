# COLORED BUSINESS AND PROFESSIONAL MEN'S ASSOCIATION FORMED IN NEW BEDFORD, MASS.

At noon, October 10, a midday luncheon of the Colored Business and Professional Mens Association was held at the Magnolia House. Ergar P. Benjamin of Boston delivered an address.

Mr Benjamin is a banker and president of the South End Board of Trade of Boston and a practicing attorney for many years in the city of Boston.

Concerning this association, I wish to say that many of its members are Garveyites, being members of New Bedford Division No. 180.

The Magnolia House is owned by Mr William West, one of our members. Mr Fred F Bonner is the head of the organization. Mr Dunbar Harris local undertaker, is vice-president, an' Dr Parham Harvard, graduate in dentistry, is its secretary-treasurer. Each is a member of the U N. I A.

The object of this association is to stimulate business development among our people and place New Bedford abroad of current enterprise in larger cities.

Already their good work has been noticed in the buying of real estate.

The program of lectures for the first half of the winter season follows: October 24. Rev J M Henderson; "Co-operation"; November 14, M J. Lehay, "Merchandising"; November 28, Ellery Vogle, "Negotiable Instruments"; December 12, Ralph Purrington, "Advertising"; December 26, A H Andrews, "The General Business Outlook."

This is the spirit that Garvey wants instilled in every Negro—business. In a few years from now may great good be accomplished.

Our Division Delegate, Mr. Joseph Williams (New York Convention, 1922), is also a member of this association, as is Dr. E. D. Osborne, who was at one time a member of the New York Branch of the U. N. I A, but after a Division was started here he transferred his membership to Division No. 180.

During the month of November we shall endeavor to mall an article to be published in The Negro World concerning a "special session" of our Division to be held in the near future.

I remain, yours for African redemption.

LENA FORTES.
Division No. 180, New Bedford, Mass.

# U. N. I. A. TO TAKE THE LEAD IN OAKLAND, CAL

## U. N. I. A. TO TAKE THE LEAD IN OAKLAND, CAL

Last Tuesday evening at the regular business meeting of the Oakland Division of the Universal Negro Improvement Association, Local No. 188 inaugurated the plans whereby funds are to be raised for the erection and operation of a steam laundry. This local declares it is going to take the initiative for providing employment for our own men and women in this vicinity.

$8,500 drive will be launched as soon as printed matter can be secured for the information of the public.

Intense interest and whole-hearted enthusiasm were keenly in evidence at this meeting. The members of this division are literally "rarin to go." The word "can't" has been positively ignored—CAN, MUST and WILL is the slogan and motto of every member.

## THE U. N. I. A. IN KANSAS CITY. MO.

October 17, 1921

We the Executive Committee of Chapter No. 377, Kansas City, Mo., held our regular meeting Sunday afternoon. We had with us the Hon. Chas. Jackson of Kansas, who delivered one of the greatest speeches we have heard since the Hon. Marcus Garvey was here. Mr. Jackson spoke in Kansas and motored to Missouri and spoke to the crowd that had been waiting about two hours for him. We are at present small in numbers, but with Mr. Jackson with us a time or two, nothing can keep us from going over the top. Mr Jackson pleaded with the members to give to the defense fund and most everyone came forward with their money. When this wonderful young man had finished speaking, we knew he was surely sent by God. Yours in the cause. J W BROWN, Secretary

Oct. 27, 1922.

The Antilla Division is trying to hold on to the cause "Aric" as best she can with her few officers and members who still have the new spirit of Negro blood in them. In spite of the very hard and trying times that the Division is undergoing, she is determined to help put over the program for the betterment of the younger generation that is coming. With the few encouraging officers and members who are trying to hold against the ebb of the times, we put before the public for the first time in Antilla, at our Liberty Hall on October 15, a harvest festival. The organizers of the function were Miss C. Lemonious and others, and with a few more helping hands the situation was carefully handled and brought to a success. Much fruit and provisions were broth into the hall on the previous day for the harvest, such as oranges, apples, grapes, water cocoa nuts, yama, plainsat and many more things of vital importance. The service for the harvest started at 4 p. m. on Sunday, the 18th, with Mr. Wm. H. Mills chairman.

The program was as follows: Opening hymn, "Greenland's Icy Mountains"; hymn 105, from ritual, prayer by president; text for the evening by the chairman from St. Mark, 14th chapter; song by Mr. N. J. Douglas, assistant secretary, song by Miss Clemison Lemonious; speech by Levi Miller; hymn 65 from ritual, by choir, at which time the collection was taken up; song by Miss Doris McLean, song by Miss Ethel Henniss, address by Mr. C. Leon; recitation by Master Rudolph Thompson; recitation by Miss Cathene Lewin; song by Mr. Wm. N. Gardiner; TEMARKS of Thanks by the president, Mr. J. Rickards; hymn 66 from ritual, by choir, next a hearty vote of thanks by the audience to the chairman, Mr. W. H. Mills, "Ethiopia's Anthem," and the function closed until the return for the mass meeting the same night. On Monday night, the 16th, the disposal of the harvest took place, which was well attended by the folks around, who patronized by buying and dancing also, about which we have to thank the audience for their behavior. Most of the articles were disposed of that night, in spite of the very hard times that are now passing in Cuba.

On the 18th one of our members, Mr. Ezekiel Hayles, was married to Miss Adulisa Walters, both of Jamaica, B.W. L. and their ceremonial function took place in Liberty Hall the same evening. The wedding was well attended and the hall was packed to its uttermost capacity. After the feast was over the band was in full tune to play the bridal set. They retired and returned at 8 p. m. sharp and the dancing program was on. The bridal set was most interesting to watch, as all those that took part in same are dancers from away back, namely, Mr. and Mrs. Hayles, Mr. and Mrs. J. McIntosh, Sir S. G. Hall, Mrs. G. Anderson, Mr. J. Weddeman and Mrs. Beatrice Lovelace. All are members of the U. N. I. A. and they seemed to enjoy themselves nicely. After that set was finished the bride and bridegroom retired and the hall was left open for the night that the well-wishers could enjoy themselves. The weather conditions around here are horrible, very little rain, and work is somewhat scarce, but in spite of same the division is trying to help the Parent Body in her financial distress as beat she can. Thanking you for space and time, Mr. Editor.

I am yours faithfully.

T. A. DEAN.

## THE CHARTER UNVEILED IN LORAIN, OHIO

# THE CHARTER UNVEILED IN LORAIN, OHIO

The meeting was called to order by President H. P. May at 2 o'clock, Sunday, October 1, 1921. It was opened by the Chaplain, Rev A. G. Brown, as usual, by singing "From Greenland's Ice Mountains" and prayer. After which the president announced the purpose of the meeting. This was followed by a solo from Mrs. A. G. Brown.

After which the veil was raised by two little girls, one of whom was Miss Ina Leach, standing at the right side of the Charter. The other little girl was Sarah Callway, standing at the left side. After the Charter was uncovered Miss Callway made a nice little speech before leaving the platform amid hearty applause.

Then solo by Rev J. H Holland, after which a speech was made by the Rev Threats of Cleveland, O., in which he spoke of the importance of understanding and the strength of a combined force. After the speech music was rendered by Mrs. Callway.

Mr Bowle, who is the ex-President of Barberton, O., Division, spoke, his subject being "Our Past Errors and How to Correct Them." Our last speaker was Capt Jackson of Cleveland, O., and a most masterly speaker He dwelled upon the necessity of a mighty legion and the coming generation and the duty of the present.

Then the president advised that all should take part in helping to put over this great program laid down by our great leader, whom God has blessed, and whom we should obey Inasmuch as God loves obedience, then He will direct our footsteps.

After which the secretary announced the collection and the amount The meeting was adjourned.

H. P. May, President

## NEW AND AWAKENED INTEREST IN U. N. I. A.

Through all the difficulties in our division we have come out victorious at the end. We have secured a permit second to none in the island of Cuba, which enables us to reorganize the division as a working factor by starting its membership on the 12th ultimo and on the 28th.

The doors of Liberty Hall were opened for the purpose of nominating and electing officers that were needed. The meeting then commenced with the singing of the opening ode of "From Greenland's Ice Mountains." followed by the Universal prayer

Mr. Ellia, who occupied the chair, explained for what reason we were gathered, which was mainly to nominate and elect officers, and asked that the election be conducted in an orderly manner.

Proceedings were as follows.

Mr. R. Martin was unanimously elected president. He then made a short address, promising the members to serve them faithfully, and asked cooperation from the other officers

Mr. T. Mahoney was also unanimously elected as general secretary. He made a brief speech after being welcomed to the office

Mr. C. J. Ellia and Mr. J Morgan were then nominated for treasurer. A vote was taken and Mr. Morgan was elected by a majority of four; the ballots showing 9 for Mr. Morgan and 5 for Mr. Ellia. The successful candidate spoke briefly.

Mr. Ellia was then elected first vice-president, while Mr M. B. Davenall was elected assistant secretary

The meeting was brought to a close with the singing of the Ethiopian National Anthem. The Benediction was said.

M. B. Daverall Asst Sec.

## SPIRITISTS WAR OVER JIM CROW LINE AT SESSIONS

## SPIRITISTS WAR OVER JIM

Do spirits draw the color line?
That question came up yesterday at the convention of the National Spiritualist Association when a movement for segregation of the colored members of the order was started. Fight over the proposition from all angles ensued.
Apparently a majority of the delegates believed there was no Mason-Dixon line in the astral world, for the long battle ended with the matter tabied, and the chairman. President George B Warne, threatening to appeal to the sergeant-at-arms if the hubbub did not subside.

## No Astral Color Line

All spooks look alike, so far as color is concerned, colored delegates asserted, and they contended there are no Jim Crow arrangements in heaven.

The proposition was advocated "as a matter of fact, not sentiment, which must be met, regardless of how we whites feel about it personally."

Proponents of segregation held that only by eliminating the colored spiritualists could the national organization carry its propaganda into the South. 'You know how Southerners feel they want to lynch us all when we hold mixed meetings.' shouted a New England delegate

Colored "Await Strength"

"Southerners love us," retorted a Negro. "I've held meetings in South Carolina and most of the attendance has been white."

The Rev. Bertha P Crear, colored, pastor of the Christian Spiritualist Church, Columbus, O. asked for delay "When we're a little stronger and a little more learned we'll leave," she declared. There was prolonged applause at this. The Chicago Tribune.
