About the time THE APPEAL came under its present management there came to Minneapolis the Rev John W. Dungue—a minister who in addition to spiritual counsel gave his people ideas in material development which endure to this day.

He built Bethesda Baptist Church, a beautiful place in which his people might worship. Then looking around him in the business world, saw there fields rape for development.

A few years later the service was enabled the employment of Mr. W. C. Jeffrey who is still one of its most dependable staff. Mr. Jeffrey is best known to the people of the Twin Cities as a capable musician, for years his work as director of the St. Thomas Choir and later as director of the Minneapolis Choral Study Club has brought him favorably toward our people.

Mr. Willis A. Colter and Mr. David S. Before the establishment of civil service when federal jons were the reward of political activity but few Afro-Americans were successful in attaching themselves to anything worth while. It is recalled that Mr. William Grey was connected with the railway mail service and Mr. Jeff Fite with the postal service. It is most significant that since employment is given to any on the basis of merit there has been no lack of those willing to try, and a steady increase in those who have succeeded.

Mr. George Tolbert is the only Afro-American in the railway mail service with headquarters in Minneapolis. He is steadily climbing to the top.

## EDWARD J. STEWART

## Manager of the Twin City Club, Minneapolis.

Manager of the Twin City Club, Minneapolis.

Mr. E. J. Stewart was born in Vicksburg, Miss, in 1865, with his parents he moved west to Kansas in 1879. He subsequently lived in St. Louis, Kansas City and St. Paul but finally landed in Minneapolis in July, 1885. For several years he worked at the St. Louis Hotel, Lake Minnetonka, during the summer season and in the winter acted as head waiter for the catering establishment of H. A. Maa. He passed through various stages of club room life and in 1897, he became manager of the "Twin City Club," situated on the second floor of 126 Hennepin avenue, where he has since remained. The club has for its secretary Chas. Welborne and James Branch for treasurer, and is a popular resort for the gentlemen of the city and their visiting friends. There are four billiard and pool tables in the club and a nice card room, etc. Also a barber shop.

Mr. Stewart has been quite successful in his management of the club and is just putting the finishing touches on his elegant 9-room home, 2915 Columbus avenue. The house is finished in stucco plaster and has an asbestos shingle roof, is strictly modern in every particular throughout, making it one of the handsomest of the many handsome homes of Minneapolis.

Mr. Stewart had the distinction of being the only Afro-American enumerator of Minneapolis in the census of 1900.

On Nov. 9, 1922, he was joined in wedlock to Miss Florence Johnson, and as the fruit of their union have one son, Earl, who is an exceptionally bright youngster and is the pride and joy of his parents.

## WILL M. SMITH

In Charge of the Night Division of the Post Office of Minneapolis. There is perhaps no better known citizen of the Twin Cities than Will M. Smith. Born in Cincinnati, Ohio, and educated there in her public schools and in the famous Gains' High School under the tutelage of the three educators, Prof. Peter H. Clark. He came to Minnesota especially prepare for that which has become his life work. Entering the service of the government as a postal employee, many years since, he is known to be one of Uncle Sam's most capable and trustworthy clerks. The Late Lafayette Harold Mason.

Lafayette Harold Mason was a Minnesota product. The most of his life was spent in the city of his birth, Minneapolis. He was educated in the public schools of his home city and at an early age began the study of music, a genius for which he inherited from his talented mother, Katie Mason-Smith. In his earlier years any instrument was equally welcome for him for he was able to play them all. Later, however, he coached him self to the piano, upon which his marvelous touch needed no teaching, Minneapolis gave him his first instructions. He also received some teaching from Prof. Woodruff, beginning at this time the pipe organ. Mason was one of the original band organized by Prof. Howard of Minneapolis. He played the piccolo with this organization until the passing years changed the boys into men and dissolved the band.

The charm of his music soon made openings for him in the business world. At various times he was employed in the musical departments of the most important department stores. Later still he was employed to play the perfect picture theaters, and here his perfect command of his instrument and ability to transpose at sight was a welcome boon to many traveling "illustrating" vocalists.

Unlike many talented people young Mason developed no disagreeable idiosyncrasies. Indeed a smile and a cheerful word were ever in evidence. His untimely death a few months ago will be reretted, not more for his music than for his cheery optimism, which made of every acquaintance a friend.

## MRS. NELLIE McCULLOUGH

## Leader of the Famous McCullough
Orchestra of Minneapolis.

Mrs. McCullough is a native of Minnesota, and when four years of age she was adopted by Mr. and Mrs. M.
