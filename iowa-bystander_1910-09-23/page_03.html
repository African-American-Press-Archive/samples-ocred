Foster Passes Examination.
Creston — Bernard Foster, of this city, was one of the three men out of a class of 26 who succeeded in passing the last state pharmacy examination held in Des Moines.

Correctionville.—Much interest is being shown in the county experiment farm here. A delegation of about twenty-five farmers were here recently and had Prof. G. H. Bliss of the state college at Arnes take them over the several hundred plots of corn these. Hundreds of farmers are by getting valuable information there. An instance of the value of the experiment farm is this, one Woodbury county farmer who has prided himself on his knowledge of corn, was here to see the three plots planted from his corn. While the stalks stood ten to fifteen feet high, the ears were short, immature and pointed. On either side was corn with "orter stalks" larger and more mature ears. He saw that in the same soil, exactly the same planting and cultivation, his neighbor's corn was far ahead of his. He saw the point and announced: "I guess all will have to change my variety."

There are more than 100 samples planted from over the county, from corn breeders and seed houses. The varieties best adapted to this section are therefore plainly shown, the value of such demonstrations be manifold, if the farmers will be led the lesson. Many are doing so.

New York.-Judge Martin in the United States court circuit, Monday, sentenced Charles R. Hekel, former secretary and treasurer of the American Sugar Refining company, who has been termed the "man higher up" in the sugar trust, to serve eight months in the New York penitentiary on Blackwell's island and to pay a fine of $5,000. Hekel had been convicted with others of conspiring to defraud the amount by the underweighing of sugar.

Judge Martin granted a stay of execution of the sentence pending an appeal to the United States circuit court of appeals. The court also reduced Hekel's bail, which had been $25,000 since his conviction last June, to $15,000.

Hekel's sentence is the climax in the federal government's prosecution of American Sugar Refining company officials and employees, growing out of the extensive underweighing frauds on the Williamsburg pler of the trust, brought to light by Richard Farr'sffair in 1907. For years earlier the company and Oliver Spitzer, their superintendent, were convicted on the first criminal trial in connection with the frauds, and the welglers are serving their sentence of a year in the Blackwell's island penitentiary.

Official announcement of the Seventh International Congress on Tuberculosis in the United States. Seminars from every well-established country in the world, has been made by the National Association for the Study and Prevention of Tuberculosis. The congress will be held in Rome from September 24 to 30, 1911, and will be similar in many respects to that held in Washington in the fall of 1908. The congress, which meets every three years, will be under the direct promotion of the king and queen of Italy.

An American committee of 100 will be appointed as the official representatives of the United States. Men while the National Association for the Study and Prevention of Tuberculosis is acting in that capacity and its office in New York will be the headquarters for the United States delegation. The secretary general of the congress is present in New York. As a direct result of the stimulus of the last international congress held in this country, the American committee will be able to report that the number of tuberculosis agencies in this country have been tripled in the three years. More than twice as much money is being spent in the fight against tuberculosis by private societies and institutions, and the appropriation and county has increased nearly fold. It is estimated that nearly $15,000,000 will be spent in anti tuberculosis work in 1910.
