INDIANAPOLIS, Ind. — We the trustees and building committee of Jones Tafernacle African Methodist Episcopal Zion Church, feel that a statement at this time relative to the great achievements wrought during the past two years, and nine months by our church, under the leadership of our pastor, Dr. S. D. Davis, is due the loyal membership who have stood so nobly in our efforts and the people of Indianapolis a well for they have been a great factor in the promotion of the work of our church. Our pastor found 'is, when he took charge July 1st, 1917, somewhat scattered and torn and much discouraged, and after making a quick survey of conditions he began at once to gather the membership into he fold and to reorganize the forces. This having been accomplished, a large and comprehensive program of reconstruction was inaugurated covering all phases of our church activities. Among the many things included in this program was the building of a modern structure adequate to care for the various activities of the church. Great progress was made along this time in raising large sums of money, notwithstanding the handicaps brought about by the great World's war. First, in order that the pastor and his family might be comfortably situated so that, he might be able to do his best work, a beautiful seven room modern parsonage was purchased in Hyland Place, the third week of July, 1917. A group of men was then organized which worked during the remainder of the summer months, reporting monthly and bringing in on the first of December, $722. Early in the spring of 1918, the church entered into a rally known as a "Mock Conference," closing the first of June, which netted $2,100. Our second drive for building funds was in the spring of 1919, which brought as $3,800. In the fall of the same year, 1919, another small effort was closed December 1st, netting $1,800. In January, 1919, we purchased the house and lot adjoining the church on the west, at a cost of $2,500 cash. The plans for the erection of our proposed structure on this present site were drawn more than a year ago, and $620 paid for same, and the building was to have been started early this spring. This building, when planned, would have cost at that time $45,000 but during the lape of nearly 18 months, building materials of all kinds have soared to such a dizzy height until the cost of constructing the building as planned by the architect, had climbed to $80,000. In view of these facts the pastor and his officers knowing the imperative need of a new building, began negotiating for the church building on the corner of Michigan and Blackford streets, just one block south of our present location, and after much labor and wise maneuvering, it was purchased by us on January 19th, 1920. The lot is 70 feet by 120, fronting on Blackford and Michigan streets. It is of stone and brick construction with slate roof. It has a full spacious basement with kitchen, dining room and other equipments, a chapel occupying the entire first floor with men's parlor, ladies' parlor, private Sunday school and class rooms, superintendent and teachers' private office, pastor's study and choir room. The main auditorium occupying the entire third floor except trustees' office, a seating capacity of 1,500 including the quarter balcony in the rear which will seat 400. A spacious lawn surrounds the entire building. For this church could not be built now for less than $150,000. The work of renovating and remodeling and utting the building in first-class shape begins this week, in order that we may move into our new quarters in the near future.

The old property at Blackford and North streets, consisting of a brick church and two five-room houses will be converted into modern five-room flats. All the property mentioned in this statement except the parsonage in Hyndall Place, will remain in the hands of the congregation of Jones Tafernacle. Our present drive for funds to carry forward this work will close April 21st and we are urging the membership and friends who believe in the religion of Jesus Christ, which is the only panacea for all human illls, to stand by us in this effort as they have done in the past. During the city election of the past week a deal of interest was shown by both white and black races of this city. The campaign was one of the hottest this place has ever known. The bone of contention was the name of Mr. E. L. Lambert, a race man being placed in the race for School director. Sentiment ran high, so much

so that many race men were actually afraid to vote for our candidate. Such a feeding of fliidity should not be permitted to exist in a community where men are determined, that all citizens should exercise their right of equal suffrage. While Mr. Lambert was defeated, yet he received 132 votes. This fact is proof, positive that curses are not a hopeful cause. We do not take this defeat as real; but rather as an incentive to keep on. After the election returns were made public, the same night an indignant white man began to circulate a petition asking the Board of Education to remove Prof. O. O. Nance from the principship of Lincoln School. At this writing the local papers have announced that near 200 names of voters and taxpayers had signed; but it will be noticed that only names of the people have been secured. To the certain knowledge of the reporter, no action by word or deed has been recorded or in any way made known of any misconduct on the part of Prof. Nance. He has done all in his power to fulfill his duty as principal, and should be permitted to remain as such. I call upon every right thinking man, woman and child of the race and all, friends of the Race to do all in their power to prevent any such high-handed movement. The gangsters claim; it is because of his efforts to assist in landing a Race man on the School Board. Further investigation brought to light that the disturbance grew out of the effort made last Spring to secure one or two days of each week to allow Colored children to attend school.
